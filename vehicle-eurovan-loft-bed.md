# Loft Bed Replacement System — Design Document
**Vehicle:** 1999 VW Eurovan (Winnebago pop-top retained)
**Location:** Upper pop-top bed (replacing original boards)
**Status:** Active design, decisions mostly finalized

---

# 1. Objective

Design a **modular, comfortable, low-friction sleeping surface** using removable elements that:
- Feel good under **direct skin contact**
- Require **no setup** (jump up, throw blanket, done)
- Support **modular use** (panels can double as shelves)
- Pack **cleanly and compactly**
- Avoid complexity or fragile mechanisms

---

# 2. Existing Baseline (Proven Working System)

## Current Slat Specification

Length: 42 13/16"
Width: 2 1/2"
Thickness: 3/4"
Profile: Flat with rounded edges
Material: Likely hardwood (unknown species)

## Key Behavior

- Slats are **pushed together (no gaps)**
- Used **directly under body (sometimes nude)**
- No mattress assumed (blanket only)
- System **feels good as-is**

**Conclusion:**
The current slat geometry and concept are **validated and should be preserved or closely matched**

---

# 3. Core Design Principles

## 3.1 Surface vs Structure

STRUCTURE = supports weight (thickness, stiffness)
SURFACE   = what body feels (top geometry)

**In this project:**
- Surface quality dominates
- Structure is already sufficient

---

## 3.2 Contact Geometry

Key variable:

**Contact width = slat width (front-to-back dimension)**

This determines:
- pressure distribution
- perceived smoothness
- comfort

---

## 3.3 Coverage Strategy

Two modes:

### A. Gapped system
- Requires careful spacing
- Produces uneven feel

### B. Gapless system (chosen)

Slats pushed together → continuous surface

- Eliminates gap-related discomfort

---

# 4. Material Decisions

## 4.1 Final Direction: Solid Wood (Preferred)

**Reasons:**
- Better tactile feel
- Easier to finish for skin contact
- Matches existing slats
- Lower risk

**Candidate materials:**
- Poplar (best balance)
- Maple (premium)
- Oak (acceptable, heavier)
- Teak (excellent but expensive)

---

## 4.2 Alternative: Laminated (Plywood)

**Viable if:**
- High-quality birch plywood used
- Edges properly sanded and finished

**Tradeoffs:**

| Factor | Hardwood | Plywood |
|---|---|---|
| Feel | ⭐ best | ✔ good |
| Consistency | ✔ | ⭐ best |
| Ease | ✔ | ⭐ easiest |
| Cost | ❌ higher | ✔ lower |

**Decision:** Hardwood preferred, plywood acceptable fallback

---

# 5. Geometry Decisions

## 5.1 Thickness (critical)

Target: 3/4"

**Reason:**
- Matches proven system
- Prevents perceived flex
- Eliminates need for redesign

---

## 5.2 Width (key design variable)

**Competing forces:**

| Narrow slats | Wide slats |
|---|---|
| Better comfort | Fewer pieces |
| More contact lines | Better stacking |
| More pieces | More "board-like" feel |

---

## 5.3 Final Width Recommendation

Target range: 2.5"–3"

**Rationale:**
- Matches current system (2.5")
- Allows modular grouping
- Avoids excessive piece count
- Maintains good surface feel

---

## 5.4 Length

42 13/16" (match existing)

# 7. Modular System Design

## 7.1 Grouping Strategy

4 slats per module

## Resulting module size:

| Slat width | Module depth (4 slats) |
|---|---|
| 2.5" | 10" |
| 3" | 12" |
| 3.5" | 14" |

---

## 7.2 Benefits

- Easy to handle
- Stackable
- Can be used as shelves
- Natural unit of organization

---

# 8. Folding / Packing Systems

## 8.1 Rejected: Full Roll

- bulky
- unstable
- annoying deployment

---

## 8.2 Recommended: Accordion Panels

[panel][panel][panel]

Panels:
- 4 slats each
- connected with webbing
- alternating hinge direction

**Behavior:**
- Deploy: unfolds instantly
- Store: stacks flat
- Use: modular

---

## 8.3 Webbing Design

- Connect slats within panel
- Connect panels with alternating hinges

Key variables:
- slight pre-tension
- top vs bottom placement

---

# 9. Edge Treatment

**Requirement:**

NO SHARP EDGES

**Method:**
- light sanding (120 → 220 grit)
- no router required
